# The Real Cost of Owning a Spanish Property (Annual Breakdown)

**Published: April 2026 | Reading time: 5 minutes**

When you budget for a property in Spain, most people think about the purchase price and the mortgage payment. They forget about everything else. After tracking 1,881 new build properties and their associated cost structures, we can give you a realistic annual cost breakdown that nobody in the sales process wants to discuss.

## The Example Property

Let us use a concrete example: a two-bedroom, two-bathroom new build apartment in Costa Blanca South. Purchase price: 220,000 EUR. Built area: 85 m2. Community pool. Second floor with lift. Two kilometres from the beach. This is the most common property profile in our dataset.

## Annual Costs Breakdown

### Community Fees (Comunidad de Propietarios)
**Annual cost: 960-1,680 EUR (80-140 EUR/month)**

Community fees cover maintenance of shared areas, pool upkeep, gardening, building insurance, lift maintenance, and reserve funds. New build communities typically start with lower fees that increase as initial developer subsidies expire and maintenance needs grow.

The range depends on the development's amenities. A basic community with a pool and garden runs 80-100 EUR/month. A complex with gym, multiple pools, and extensive grounds can reach 150-200 EUR/month.

### IBI (Property Tax)
**Annual cost: 350-700 EUR**

The Impuesto sobre Bienes Inmuebles is calculated on the cadastral value, not the market value. For a new build purchased at 220,000 EUR, the cadastral value is typically set at 70,000-100,000 EUR. Municipal IBI rates in Costa Blanca South range from 0.5% to 0.9% of cadastral value.

New builds sometimes benefit from a lower IBI in the first few years because the cadastral value has not yet been fully established. Expect increases as revaluations occur.

### Basura (Waste Collection)
**Annual cost: 100-250 EUR**

A separate municipal charge for rubbish collection. Not negotiable, not avoidable. Varies significantly between towns — Torrevieja charges approximately 120 EUR while some smaller municipalities charge up to 280 EUR.

### Non-Resident Income Tax (IRNR)
**Annual cost: 200-400 EUR (if not renting out)**

Even if the property sits empty, Spanish tax law imputes a nominal rental income and taxes it. For EU/EEA residents: 19% on 1.1% of cadastral value. On a cadastral value of 85,000 EUR: 85,000 x 1.1% x 19% = 177 EUR. Non-EU residents pay 24% on 2% of cadastral value: 85,000 x 2% x 24% = 408 EUR.

### Home Insurance
**Annual cost: 200-400 EUR**

Building insurance is typically included in community fees. Contents insurance is your responsibility. For a furnished two-bedroom apartment, expect 200-300 EUR for basic cover. If you are renting the property, landlord insurance with liability cover runs 350-450 EUR.

### Utilities (If Not Renting)
**Annual cost: 600-1,200 EUR**

Even empty properties incur standing charges. Electricity standing charge: approximately 15-25 EUR/month. Water standing charge: 10-20 EUR/month. If you visit periodically and use heating or air conditioning, add consumption costs.

If the property is rented, tenants typically pay utilities. But during vacancy periods, standing charges continue.

### Maintenance Reserve
**Annual cost: 1,000-2,200 EUR (budget 0.5-1% of property value)**

New builds require minimal maintenance in the first three years. But appliances fail, plumbing develops issues, and cosmetic wear accumulates. A prudent owner budgets 0.5% of property value annually in years 1-5, rising to 1% from year 6 onwards.

Common maintenance items: air conditioning service (100-200 EUR annually), water heater maintenance (80-150 EUR), pest treatment in summer (50-80 EUR), and periodic deep cleaning (150-300 EUR if between rental guests).

### Property Management (If Renting)
**Annual cost: 15-25% of rental income**

If you rent the property through a management company, fees typically run 18-22% of gross rental income for short-term rentals (including marketing, booking management, key handover, cleaning coordination, and guest communication). Long-term rental management is cheaper at 8-12%.

On estimated gross rental income of 13,500 EUR (6.2% gross yield on 220,000 EUR), management fees would be 2,430-3,375 EUR.

## Costs That Catch People Off Guard

**Special assessments (derramas).** The community may vote to levy a one-off charge for major repairs or improvements — new lifts, facade repairs, pool renovation. These can run 1,000-5,000 EUR per owner and are not optional if approved by the community vote.

**Tax filing costs.** Non-residents must file Spanish tax returns. A gestor or tax advisor charges 150-300 EUR per year for this service.

**Currency exchange costs.** If your income is in GBP, NOK, SEK, or another non-EUR currency, exchange rate fluctuations and transfer fees add 1-3% to every transaction. Use a specialist currency broker rather than your bank to minimize costs.

**Return flights.** If you visit the property 4-6 times per year, budget for flights and car rental. From Northern Europe, this adds 2,000-4,000 EUR annually. Not technically a property cost, but a real expense that should enter your total return calculation.

## The Honest Picture

Owning property in Spain is not expensive by Northern European standards, but it is not negligible either. The annual running costs for a typical new build apartment are 3,500-4,000 EUR for an empty property and 7,500-8,500 EUR for a managed rental. Factor these numbers into your investment analysis before committing.
