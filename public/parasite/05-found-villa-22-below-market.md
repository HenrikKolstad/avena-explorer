# How We Found a Villa Priced 22% Below Market Using Data Screening

**Published: April 2026 | Reading time: 5 minutes**

Property searching in Spain usually follows a predictable pattern: you contact an agent, they show you what earns them the highest commission, and you choose from a curated selection that may not represent the best value. We took a different approach. Using quantitative screening across 1,881 new build properties, we identified listings where the asking price sits significantly below what our regression model predicts. Here is one case study that illustrates the methodology.

## The Screening Process

Our scoring engine at Avena Terminal evaluates every new build listing across coastal Spain using a hedonic regression model. The model estimates what a property "should" cost based on its observable characteristics: location, size, number of bedrooms, property type, energy rating, developer track record, beach proximity, and several other variables.

When a property's asking price sits meaningfully below the model's predicted price, it represents a potential value opportunity. The key word is "potential" — there may be legitimate reasons for the lower price that the model does not capture (construction delays, poor orientation, noisy location). But the model identifies candidates for further investigation.

## The Property

In February 2026, our screening flagged a three-bedroom villa in a Costa Blanca South municipality. The key metrics:

- **Asking price:** 289,000 EUR
- **Model predicted price:** 371,000 EUR
- **Discount to model:** 22.1%
- **Price per square metre:** 1,925 EUR/m2 (town average for comparable villas: 2,480 EUR/m2)
- **Estimated gross rental yield:** 7.4%
- **Avena Score:** 82/100
- **Plot size:** 220 m2
- **Built area:** 150 m2
- **Energy rating:** B
- **Community pool:** Yes
- **Distance to beach:** 2.8 km

## Why Was It Priced Low?

Investigation revealed several factors:

**Phase pricing strategy.** The villa was in Phase 1 of a larger development with 40 planned units across three phases. The developer priced Phase 1 aggressively to build momentum and secure early sales, planning to increase prices by 12-15% in subsequent phases. This is a common strategy, but the discount it creates is real if you buy in Phase 1.

**Developer portfolio pressure.** This developer had three concurrent projects completing in 2026. Selling Phase 1 quickly freed up working capital for the other projects. The pricing reflected financial pressure rather than a quality deficit.

**Limited marketing.** The development had minimal online presence. No professional photographs, basic listing descriptions, and no social media marketing. Properties that are not marketed effectively tend to attract fewer buyers, which keeps prices competitive. Our data scraping captures these listings even when they lack visibility on mainstream portals.

## How the Score Breaks Down

The Avena Score of 82/100 decomposed as follows:

- **Value (40% weight): 94/100** — The asking price sat 22% below the regression model's predicted price, placing it in the top 5% for value across the dataset.
- **Yield (25% weight): 78/100** — The estimated 7.4% gross yield is above average for villas in this price range. Comparable rental data for the town supported 750-850 EUR per week in peak season.
- **Location (20% weight): 68/100** — The 2.8 km beach distance is not walkable, which limits the location score. However, the town has good amenities, a hospital, and international schools.
- **Quality (10% weight): 72/100** — B energy rating, reputable developer with 8 completed projects, and standard specification including pre-installed air conditioning and fitted kitchen.
- **Risk (5% weight): 70/100** — Phase 1 of a new development carries some risk, but the developer's track record and the project's bank guarantee mitigated concerns.

## What Made This a Good Deal

Beyond the raw discount, several factors supported the value thesis:

**Comparable transactions confirmed the gap.** Recent completed sales in the same town for similar villas ranged from 340,000 to 395,000 EUR. The 289,000 EUR asking price was not a data error — it reflected genuine developer pricing strategy.

**The rental market was deep.** The town had over 200 active short-term rental listings on major platforms, indicating established tourist demand. Towns with fewer than 50 active rental listings carry higher vacancy risk.

**Infrastructure investment was underway.** The municipality had approved a 12 million EUR improvement plan for the beachfront promenade and access roads, scheduled for completion in 2027. This type of public investment historically supports property value growth.

## Lessons From This Example

Several principles emerge from this case study:

**Screen broadly, investigate narrowly.** Looking at 1,881 properties through a quantitative lens identified perhaps 50-80 genuinely interesting opportunities. Manual research on each of those candidates revealed which discounts were justified by genuine value and which reflected hidden problems.

**Developer motivation matters.** Understanding why a property is priced below market is as important as identifying the discount. Phase pricing, portfolio pressure, and limited marketing are benign reasons. Construction defects, legal disputes, or planning issues are not.

**Early phases offer real value.** If you can tolerate the uncertainty of buying into Phase 1 of a new development — including potential construction delays and living on a building site while later phases complete — the pricing advantage can be substantial.

**Data does not replace due diligence.** The scoring model identifies candidates. A lawyer, a surveyor, and an in-person visit are still essential. No model captures everything. But starting with data narrows your search from thousands of listings to a manageable shortlist.
