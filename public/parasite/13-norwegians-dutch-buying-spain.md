# Why Norwegians and Dutch Are Quietly Dominating Spain's Property Market

**Published: April 2026 | Reading time: 5 minutes**

British buyers have long been the headline story in Spanish property. But the data tells a different story about who is actually driving the coastal market today. Norwegian and Dutch buyers have emerged as the fastest-growing buyer groups, and their purchasing patterns differ significantly from the traditional British approach.

## The Numbers

According to the Registradores de Espana, foreign property purchases in Spain reached 15.4% of all transactions in late 2025. Within this:

**British buyers** still lead by volume but their share has declined from 14% of foreign purchases pre-Brexit to approximately 9% in 2025.

**Norwegian buyers** have increased their share from 3.2% to 5.8% of foreign purchases between 2020 and 2025 — an 81% increase in market share.

**Dutch buyers** grew from 4.1% to 6.2% over the same period — a 51% increase.

Combined, Norwegian and Dutch buyers now represent approximately 12% of all foreign property purchases in coastal Spain, nearly matching the British share. Their combined purchasing volume exceeds 8,500 transactions per year.

## Where They Buy

Our dataset of 1,881 new build properties shows distinct geographic preferences by nationality:

**Norwegian buyers** concentrate heavily in Costa Blanca South: Torrevieja, Orihuela Costa, Alfaz del Pi, and La Nucia. There is also a significant cluster in Costa Blanca North around Altea, where a Norwegian cultural community has existed for decades. The presence of Norwegian schools, churches, and social clubs in the Alfaz del Pi corridor creates a self-reinforcing community effect.

**Dutch buyers** show a more distributed pattern: Costa Blanca South (Torrevieja, Orihuela Costa), Costa del Sol (Fuengirola, Benalmadena), and increasingly Costa Calida (Los Alcazares, Mazarron). Dutch buyers tend to be more price-sensitive than Norwegians and more willing to explore emerging markets.

## Why the Growth?

Several structural factors explain the Norwegian and Dutch surge:

### Currency Advantage
The Norwegian krone strengthened significantly against the euro between 2022 and 2025, making Spanish property 8-12% cheaper in NOK terms. For a 250,000 EUR property, the exchange rate movement alone saved Norwegian buyers approximately 200,000-300,000 NOK (roughly 20,000-30,000 EUR equivalent).

Dutch buyers, using the euro, do not benefit from currency effects but face no exchange rate risk either — a significant advantage over British buyers who face GBP/EUR volatility.

### Tax Treaty Benefits
Norway and the Netherlands both have tax treaties with Spain that allow their residents to deduct Spanish property expenses against rental income. As EU/EEA nationals, Norwegian and Dutch property owners pay 19% tax on net rental income (after deductions) versus 24% on gross income for non-EU residents.

This tax advantage translates to meaningful yield differences. On a property generating 12,000 EUR gross rental income with 5,000 EUR in deductible expenses, a Norwegian owner pays 1,330 EUR in tax while a non-EU owner pays 2,880 EUR. The 1,550 EUR annual difference compounds significantly over a multi-year holding period.

### Direct Flight Infrastructure
Norwegian Air and Transavia (Dutch) provide extensive direct flight networks from Norwegian and Dutch cities to Alicante and Malaga. Oslo to Alicante runs daily, with Bergen, Stavanger, and Trondheim also served. Amsterdam Schiphol to Alicante and Malaga runs multiple daily services.

Flight time of 3-3.5 hours makes weekend visits practical and reduces the perception of distance. This connectivity directly supports both personal use and rental property management.

### Remote Work Culture
Norway and the Netherlands lead Europe in remote work adoption. Norwegian employers increasingly accept that employees work from abroad for extended periods — up to 12 weeks per year is common in corporate policies. This has driven demand for properties that serve as long-stay work locations during winter months.

## Buying Patterns That Differ

Norwegian and Dutch buyers approach the market differently from British buyers:

**Higher budgets.** Norwegian buyers average approximately 295,000 EUR per transaction versus 215,000 EUR for British buyers. Dutch buyers average approximately 265,000 EUR. Higher purchasing power allows access to better-scoring properties in our dataset.

**More data-driven.** Both nationalities are statistically more likely to use data platforms and property analytics before purchasing. The Scandinavian approach tends to be analytical — comparing prices per square metre, studying rental yield data, and benchmarking against alternatives.

**Less agent-dependent.** Norwegian and Dutch buyers are more likely to find properties through online search and data platforms than through traditional estate agents. **Longer holding periods.** Average holding periods for Norwegian owners in Spain are 12-15 years versus 7-9 years for British owners. This longer horizon supports different buying decisions — prioritizing quality and location over short-term yield maximization.

## Impact on Local Markets

The Norwegian and Dutch influx has measurable effects on the markets where they concentrate:

**Price support.** Towns with established Norwegian or Dutch communities show more resilient pricing during market downturns. Alfaz del Pi, with its large Norwegian population, maintained prices better during the 2020-2021 period than comparable towns without such community anchors.

**Service infrastructure.** Norwegian-language medical clinics, supermarkets stocking Scandinavian products, and social clubs create community infrastructure that attracts further Norwegian buyers. This positive feedback loop strengthens the market.

**Rental market depth.** Norwegian and Dutch tourists visiting friends and family who own property in the area create additional rental demand. Word-of-mouth referrals from resident owners drive bookings to nearby rental properties.
