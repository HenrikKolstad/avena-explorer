# Spanish New Build Prices Are 19% Below Peak — What That Actually Means

**Published: April 2026 | Reading time: 5 minutes**

The headline statistic from our analysis of 1,881 new build properties across coastal Spain is that asking prices average 19% below peak resale valuations. This figure has generated attention, but it requires context. Here is what the number means, where it comes from, and why it matters for buyers.

## How We Calculate the Discount

The 19% figure is derived from comparing current new build asking prices against peak resale transaction prices recorded by the Registradores de Espana (Spain's property registry) for equivalent properties in the same municipality. "Equivalent" means same property type, similar size (within 15% of floor area), and same general location within the town.

The Registradores data represents actual completed transactions, not asking prices. This distinction matters because asking prices and transaction prices typically differ by 5-10% in Spain, with sellers accepting less than their listed price. Our comparison uses the registry's transaction data, which is the most reliable price benchmark available in the Spanish market.

The methodology uses hedonic regression — a statistical technique that controls for observable property characteristics (bedrooms, size, location, amenities) to isolate the pure price difference between new builds and peak resale values. The model includes town dummy variables to account for location-specific pricing, and property-type controls to ensure we compare like with like.

## Regional Variation

The 19% average conceals significant regional variation:

**Costa Blanca South: 22-28% below peak.** This is the widest gap in our dataset. Towns like Torrevieja, Orihuela Costa, and Pilar de la Horadada show the largest discounts, driven by substantial new supply entering the market from 2023-2024 building permits. Developer competition is intense here, with multiple projects competing for the same buyer pool.

**Costa Calida: 25-34% below peak.** The widest discounts in absolute terms, but from a lower base. Peak prices in Costa Calida were already below the other costas, so the absolute euro difference is smaller despite the larger percentage gap.

**Costa Blanca North: 12-18% below peak.** The premium end of the Costa Blanca market shows a tighter gap. Towns like Javea, Moraira, and Altea have stronger underlying demand and less new supply, keeping prices closer to historical peaks.

**Costa del Sol: 10-15% below peak.** The tightest gap reflects the Costa del Sol's brand premium and the strongest international demand of any Spanish coastal region. Marbella specifically shows only a 6-9% gap, essentially at equilibrium.

## Why the Gap Exists

Several structural factors explain why new build prices sit below peak resale values:

**Supply timing.** Building permits issued during 2022-2024 are delivering properties into a market where demand growth has been steady but not explosive. Unlike the pre-2008 boom, when speculative demand absorbed supply instantly, today's buyers are more cautious and better informed.

**Developer financing pressures.** Developers who financed projects at 2022-2023 interest rates face carrying costs that incentivize selling at competitive prices rather than holding for higher margins. This is particularly true for mid-market developments where margins are thinner.

**Information asymmetry reduction.** Platforms like [avenaterminal.com](https://avenaterminal.com) and other property data tools have made pricing more transparent. Buyers can now compare asking prices across developments and towns, creating competitive pressure that keeps prices honest.

**Buyer demographic shift.** The dominant buyer profile for Spanish new builds has shifted from speculative investors to end-users and buy-to-let investors who model returns carefully. These buyers anchor their price expectations to rental yields and comparable data rather than aspirational resale values.

## What It Means for Buyers

A 19% discount relative to peak does not necessarily mean you are getting a bargain. It means several things:

**The market has not recovered to its peak.** Spanish property prices nationally have recovered to approximately 90% of their 2007 peak in nominal terms. Adjusted for inflation, prices remain substantially below peak. New builds in coastal areas, which were among the most overvalued during the boom, still have further to recover.

**You are buying at a competitive price point.** If peak prices represented fair value (debatable), then current prices offer a genuine discount. If peak prices were inflated (likely in many markets), then the current 19% gap may represent a correction toward fair value rather than a discount below it.

**Regional analysis matters more than national averages.** The fact that Costa del Sol shows only a 10-15% gap while Costa Calida shows 25-34% tells you these are fundamentally different markets operating on different dynamics. Treating them as a single market will lead to poor decisions.

## Is This Discount Sustainable?

Our view, based on supply pipeline data and demand trends, is that the discount will narrow over the next 2-3 years in most markets. Building permit volumes have declined from their 2023 peak, meaning new supply entering the market in 2027-2028 will be lower. Meanwhile, demand from Northern European buyers continues to grow, supported by remote work trends and favorable EUR exchange rates for Scandinavian and British buyers.

The narrowing will not be uniform. Costa del Sol will likely reach parity with peak prices first. Costa Blanca South and Costa Calida will retain some discount for longer due to higher supply volumes and less constrained development land.

## The Practical Takeaway

The 19% headline number is real, but it is an average across 1,881 properties in 100 towns. Your specific opportunity depends on your target town, property type, and budget. Use data, not averages, to make your decision.
