# The Foreigner's Guide to Buying a New Build in Spain (Step by Step)

**Published: April 2026 | Reading time: 6 minutes**

Buying a new build property in Spain as a foreigner is straightforward if you follow the correct process. It is also easy to get wrong if you rely on the developer's guidance alone. After tracking 1,881 new build properties across coastal Spain and speaking with hundreds of international buyers, here is the complete process.

## Step 1: Get Your NIE Number

The Numero de Identificacion de Extranjero is mandatory for any financial transaction in Spain. You cannot buy property, open a bank account, or sign a contract without it.

**How to apply:** At a Spanish police station (Oficina de Extranjeros) in person, or at a Spanish consulate in your home country. You need your passport, a completed EX-15 form, proof of why you need the NIE (a letter from the developer or your lawyer stating your intention to purchase), and the fee receipt (approximately 12 EUR, paid at a bank).

**Processing time:** 1-4 weeks in Spain; 2-8 weeks at consulates abroad. Some consulates now offer digital processing.

**Practical tip:** Apply for your NIE before you go property shopping. Having it ready speeds up everything else by weeks.

## Step 2: Open a Spanish Bank Account

While not strictly mandatory, a Spanish bank account is strongly recommended. Spanish direct debits (domiciliacion) are the standard payment method for community fees, utilities, IBI tax, and insurance.

Most Spanish banks require an in-person visit to open an account, though some now offer video identification for EU residents. Bring your passport, NIE, proof of address in your home country, and proof of income.

**Bank recommendation:** Choose a bank with online banking in English and reasonable international transfer fees. Expect to pay 50-200 EUR annually in maintenance fees.

## Step 3: Find the Right Property

This is where most buyers make their first mistake: they visit Spain, contact an agent, and buy whatever they are shown on a three-day viewing trip. A better approach is to screen the market quantitatively before visiting.

Our dataset on [avenaterminal.com](https://avenaterminal.com) tracks 1,881 new build properties across 100 towns in four coastal regions. Each listing is scored from 0-100 based on value, yield, location, quality, and risk. You can filter by town, property type, budget range, and minimum score to create a shortlist before booking flights.

Key questions to answer during screening:
- What is the price per square metre relative to the town average?
- What is the estimated rental yield?
- How does the developer's track record look?
- What is the property's energy rating?
- How far is it from the beach, airport, and essential services?

## Step 4: Hire an Independent Lawyer

This is non-negotiable. Never use the developer's lawyer or the agent's recommended lawyer. Your lawyer must be independent and work exclusively for your interests.

**What they should do:** Verify the developer's building license, check for planning issues, confirm the bank guarantee (aval bancario) is in place, review the specification and contract, manage the nota simple (property registry check), handle the power of attorney if you cannot attend completion in person.

**Cost:** 1-1.5% of the purchase price, typically 2,000-4,000 EUR for a standard new build transaction.

**How to find one:** Ask for recommendations from your national expat association, not from the agent selling you the property.

## Step 5: The Reservation

Once you have identified a property, the process begins with a reservation fee. This is typically 3,000-10,000 EUR and takes the property off the market while your lawyer conducts due diligence. The reservation fee should be refundable if due diligence reveals problems — ensure this is written into the reservation agreement.

**Timeline:** Due diligence should take 2-4 weeks. If your lawyer needs more time, extend the reservation period rather than rushing.

## Step 6: The Purchase Contract (Contrato de Compraventa)

Once due diligence is clear, you sign the purchase contract. Key elements:

**Payment schedule:** For off-plan or under-construction properties, payments are staged — typically 30-40% on contract signing, further installments during construction, and the balance (30-40%) at completion. For key-ready properties, you pay 10% deposit on contract and the balance at notary completion.

**Bank guarantee (aval bancario):** Spanish law requires developers to guarantee all stage payments with a bank or insurance guarantee. This means if the developer goes bankrupt before completing the project, your money is returned by the guarantor. Your lawyer must verify this guarantee exists before you pay anything beyond the reservation.

**Specification:** The contract should include a detailed specification document listing every material, finish, and fixture included in the property. Review this carefully — disputes about specification are the most common post-completion complaint.

## Step 7: Construction Monitoring (Off-Plan Only)

If buying off-plan, you should monitor construction progress. Some buyers visit quarterly; others rely on lawyer updates or developer photos. Key milestones to track: foundation completion, structural frame, roof installation, window fitting, interior finishing, and snag list.

**Completion delays:** Delays of 3-6 months are common in Spanish new build construction. Your contract should specify the expected completion date and provide for compensation if the delay exceeds a defined period (typically 6-12 months).

## Step 8: Pre-Completion Inspection (Snag List)

Before completion, you have the right to inspect the property and compile a snag list (lista de defectos). Bring a builder or surveyor if possible. Common snags in Spanish new builds include: poorly aligned tiles, leaking windows, electrical faults, plumbing issues, and finishing defects.

The developer must rectify snag list items before or shortly after completion. Do not sign the completion documents until critical snags are addressed or a written commitment to fix them is in place.

## Step 9: Completion at the Notary

Completion takes place at a Spanish notary (notario). Present: buyer (or power of attorney), developer representative, and the notary. The notary reads the deed (escritura), both parties sign, and the balance of the purchase price is paid (usually by bank transfer or banker's draft).

On the same day or shortly after, you pay:
- **IVA (10%)** to the developer
- **AJD (1.2-1.5%)** to the tax office
- **Notary fees (600-1,200 EUR)**
- **Land registry fees (400-700 EUR)**

Your lawyer can handle all of this via power of attorney if you cannot attend in person.

## Step 10: Post-Completion Administration

After completion, several administrative tasks remain:

- **Register the property** at the Land Registry (Registro de la Propiedad)
- **Set up utility contracts** (electricity, water, gas, internet)
- **Register for IBI** at the town hall
- **Set up community fee payments** via direct debit
- **Arrange insurance** (building and contents)
- **Apply for a tourist license** if you plan to rent short-term
- **Register with the tax office** for non-resident tax obligations

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

Based on patterns we see across our 1,881 tracked properties:

**Buying without data.** Too many buyers choose based on a gut feeling after a viewing trip. Use quantitative tools like [avenaterminal.com](https://avenaterminal.com) to benchmark any property against the broader market before committing.

**Skipping the lawyer.** The 2,000-4,000 EUR legal fee is the best investment in the entire process.

**Ignoring ongoing costs.** Budget 3,500-4,000 EUR per year in running costs for an empty property, and 7,500-8,500 EUR for a managed rental.

**Assuming resale liquidity.** Spanish property is not as liquid as Northern European markets. Selling can take 6-18 months. Buy with a long-term horizon.
