# This Town Keeps Appearing at the Top of My Algorithm. Here Is Why.

Every week I review the output of the scoring model at avenaterminal.com. I look at which properties score highest, which towns cluster at the top, and whether any patterns have shifted.

One town keeps appearing disproportionately in the top 50 scored properties. I am not going to name it directly in this post -- the point is not to pump a specific location, but to explain the characteristics that make certain towns algorithmically attractive.

If you understand why the model favors certain places, you can apply the same logic to any town you are evaluating.

## The Profile of a High-Scoring Town

Our model evaluates five dimensions: Value (40%), Yield (25%), Location (20%), Quality (10%), and Risk (5%). A town that scores well needs to perform across multiple dimensions simultaneously. Here is what that looks like in practice.

### Underpriced Relative to Neighbors

The town in question has average asking prices approximately 20-25% below neighboring towns with similar beach access, infrastructure, and climate. This is not because the town is worse -- it is because the neighboring towns have been more heavily marketed to international buyers over the past decade.

Marketing spend drives awareness. Awareness drives demand. Demand drives prices. A town that has been overlooked by the major international agents and property shows ends up with lower prices despite having comparable fundamentals.

Our hedonic model picks this up automatically. When the town dummy coefficient is low relative to the property characteristics on offer, properties in that town score highly on the Value dimension.

### Strong Rental Demand Relative to Supply

This is the critical factor for yield. The town has a growing tourist base -- particularly from Scandinavian and Northern European visitors -- but a limited supply of quality short-term rentals. The result: higher occupancy and higher nightly rates relative to purchase prices.

I verified this by pulling comparable Airbnb listings in the town versus neighboring, more expensive towns. The nightly rates are only 10-15% lower than the pricier neighbors, but purchase prices are 20-25% lower. That gap is what produces the yield premium.

On avenaterminal.com, we calculate yield estimates using these actual comparables, not theoretical projections. The yield numbers for this town are consistently 1-2 percentage points above the regional average.

### Infrastructure on the Upswing

The town has seen recent investment in road connections, commercial amenities, and healthcare facilities. A new health center opened last year. A commercial zone is expanding. Airport transfer time has improved with road upgrades.

These improvements do not appear in most property analyses because they are forward-looking and qualitative. But they matter enormously for long-term value. Towns that are investing in infrastructure tend to see price appreciation as livability improves and more buyers discover them.

Our model captures this partially through the location quality dimension, but I will be honest: it is one area where human judgment still supplements the algorithm.

### Reasonable Developer Quality

The developments in this town come from mid-tier developers with solid track records. Not the luxury mega-developers of the Costa del Sol, but builders who have completed multiple projects on time and to specification.

This matters for the Risk dimension of the score. A beautifully priced property from an unknown developer with no completion history scores lower than a slightly more expensive property from a proven builder.

In this town, the developer quality is consistent. You are not taking a gamble on whether the project gets finished.

## Why This Pattern Repeats

The algorithm is not magic. It favors the intersection of low prices, high yields, decent locations, and manageable risk. Towns that hit all four criteria tend to cluster at the top of every ranking we run.

The interesting question is: why do these opportunities persist? If the data clearly shows that Town X is undervalued, why have prices not adjusted?

Three reasons:

**Awareness lag.** Most international buyers still discover properties through agents and property shows, not data platforms. The agents promote the towns where they have developer relationships and commission structures. Data-driven discovery is still new.

**Anchoring bias.** Buyers who have decided they want to be in a well-known resort town will not consider an alternative 15 km down the road, even if the numbers are dramatically better. Brand recognition in real estate is powerful.

**Information cost.** Until recently, comparing towns at this level of granularity required pulling data from multiple sources, running your own analysis, and verifying rental figures independently. Most buyers do not have the time or skills to do this. That is exactly why I built avenaterminal.com.

## How to Find Your Own High-Scoring Town

Here is the framework:

1. Filter our database by region and budget.
2. Sort by overall score or yield.
3. Look for clusters -- towns where multiple properties score above 75.
4. Research the town independently. Visit, talk to locals, check infrastructure plans.
5. Verify rental figures against Airbnb listings in the area.
