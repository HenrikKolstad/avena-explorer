# Spanish Property Market April 2026: What the Data Shows

Every quarter I pull the latest numbers from our database at avenaterminal.com and look for trends. April 2026 shows some interesting shifts that I think buyers and investors should know about.

This is not a market forecast. I do not predict prices. What I do is track 1,881 new build listings across Costa Blanca, Costa Calida, and Costa del Sol, and report what the data actually shows right now.

## Prices Are Up, But Unevenly

Across all regions, average asking prices for new builds have increased roughly 4-6% year-over-year. But the averages mask significant variation.

Costa del Sol has seen the largest increases, with some premium towns showing 8-10% price growth. This is partly driven by continued international demand -- particularly from Scandinavian and Northern European buyers -- and partly by rising construction costs feeding through to asking prices.

Costa Blanca North has been relatively stable, with 3-4% growth. The market here is mature and prices already reflect the lifestyle premium that the region commands.

The most interesting story is Costa Blanca South and Costa Calida. Prices have risen 5-7%, but from a lower base. More importantly, the number of new listings entering the market has increased. Developers are clearly seeing opportunity in these regions, which means more supply and potentially more competition for buyer attention.

## The Yield Compression Problem

As prices rise, rental yields compress. This is straightforward mathematics: if rents grow slower than purchase prices, the yield percentage falls.

In our data, gross rental yield estimates have declined by approximately 0.5-0.8 percentage points compared to six months ago across the most popular towns. A property that would have shown 8% gross yield in October 2025 now shows closer to 7.2-7.5% at the higher asking price.

The exceptions are towns where rental demand is strengthening. A handful of locations -- typically those with improving infrastructure, new commercial development, or growing airline route connectivity -- have seen rental rates rise in step with purchase prices, maintaining their yield profiles.

These are the towns that score highest in our model on avenaterminal.com, and they are where I would focus attention if yield is your primary investment criterion.

## Off-Plan Discounts Are Shrinking

Six months ago, off-plan properties traded at a roughly 12-15% discount to completed equivalents. That gap has narrowed to 8-12%.

Two factors drive this. First, buyer confidence in the new build market has improved, reducing the risk premium that off-plan traditionally carries. Second, developers have noticed that off-plan units are selling faster and have adjusted pricing upward.

For buyers, this means the off-plan arbitrage -- buying early at a significant discount and benefiting from appreciation during construction -- is still available but less generous than it was.

## What I Am Watching

**Construction cost trajectory.** Spanish construction costs have stabilized after two years of increases, but they have not declined. If input costs hold, asking prices are unlikely to fall in the near term. If costs start rising again, expect another round of upward price adjustments.

**Remote worker demand.** The digital nomad visa and expanding remote work culture continue to drive demand from a younger buyer demographic. These buyers tend to prioritize yield and connectivity over traditional resort amenities, which is shifting demand toward different towns than the traditional retirement market favored.

**Interest rates.** The ECB's rate path matters less for new build buyers (many pay cash or use non-Spanish financing) but affects the broader Spanish property market. Lower rates support prices across the board.

**Supply pipeline.** The number of new developments breaking ground has increased in Costa Blanca South and Costa Calida. This new supply will take 18-24 months to reach the market. When it does, it could moderate price growth in those regions.

## The Data-Driven Approach

One thing I have learned from tracking this market daily: aggregate trends matter less than property-level analysis. The Spanish coastal market is not one market -- it is dozens of micro-markets, each with different supply dynamics, demand profiles, and yield characteristics.

A 5% average price increase means nothing if the specific town you are looking at has seen 10% increases or 2% decreases. The average masks the signal.

This is why I built avenaterminal.com to work at the individual property level. Every listing has its own score, its own yield estimate, its own price comparison. The macro trends provide context, but the buy or don't buy decision should be driven by property-specific data.

If you are evaluating the Spanish market right now, I would encourage you to look past the headlines and into the numbers. The opportunities are still there, but they require more precision to find than they did a year ago.
