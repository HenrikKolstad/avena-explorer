# I Left Traditional Real Estate Behind. The Industry Did Not Like That.

Six months ago I stopped recommending that friends ask real estate agents for property advice in Spain. Instead, I told them to look at the data first. That made me unpopular with a few people in the industry.

The reaction was instructive. Not because anyone disagreed with the data -- they could not, because the data is public and verifiable. They were upset because transparency threatens a business model built on information asymmetry.

Let me explain what I mean.

## The Information Gap Is the Product

In most consumer markets, the internet has collapsed information asymmetry. You can compare car prices across every dealer in the country. You can read reviews of every restaurant. You can see the historical price of almost any publicly traded asset.

Spanish coastal real estate is different. Try to find out what properties in a specific town actually sold for last year. Try to compare the asking price of a new build to the average price per square meter in that postcode. Try to calculate whether a developer's quoted rental yield is realistic given actual occupancy data.

You will struggle. Not because the data does not exist, but because nobody has assembled it in a way that buyers can use. The agents have fragments. The developers have their own internal numbers. The MLS feeds are accessible but not analyzed.

This gap is not an accident. It is a feature of how the market operates. When buyers cannot compare, they cannot negotiate effectively. When they cannot verify yield claims, they accept them. When they cannot see price dispersion within a town, they trust whoever is sitting across the table.

## Why I Built Something Different

I built avenaterminal.com because I was frustrated as a buyer, not because I wanted to disrupt an industry. I wanted to know: is this property fairly priced? What is the realistic rental yield? How does it compare to every other option in this town and price range?

Those questions should be trivially easy to answer in 2026. For stocks, they are. For property in Torrevieja or Orihuela Costa, they were not.

So I assembled the data. 1,881 new build properties across three coastal regions. Each one scored on a 0-100 scale using hedonic regression, rental yield modeling, and location analysis. Every assumption documented. Every data source cited.

## The Pushback

The response from buyers has been overwhelmingly positive. People who are spending 200,000+ EUR on a property genuinely want to understand what they are buying. They want to see price comparisons. They want independent yield estimates. They want to know if they are looking at a 60-score property or an 85-score property.

The response from parts of the industry has been less enthusiastic. A few agents have told me -- politely and less politely -- that scoring properties publicly undermines their ability to sell. That buyers who see a low score will walk away from properties that are actually good value.

My response: if a property scores low, there is a reason. Maybe the price is above the town average. Maybe the yield estimate is below the regional benchmark. The score does not say do not buy. It says here is what the data shows, and these are the trade-offs.

Any agent who is selling fairly priced properties with realistic yield expectations should welcome transparency. It validates their recommendations. The only people threatened by data are those whose recommendations do not survive contact with it.

## What Transparency Looks Like in Practice

On avenaterminal.com, every property listing shows:

- The asking price and how it compares to the model's estimate for that town and type
- Estimated gross and net rental yield based on comparable short-term rental data
- Price per square meter relative to the town average
- A composite score with a breakdown across five dimensions
- Beach distance, pool type, construction status

Nothing is hidden behind a contact form. There is no requirement to provide your phone number before seeing prices. The data is the product, and it is accessible to everyone.

## Where This Goes

I am not trying to replace agents. Good agents provide local knowledge, legal guidance, and transaction support that no algorithm can replicate. What I am trying to replace is the information vacuum that buyers currently operate in.

The future of real estate -- in Spain and everywhere -- is data-first. Buyers will arrive at viewings already knowing the fair price range, the realistic yield, and the comparative ranking of the property they are viewing. Agents who adapt to informed buyers will thrive. Those who rely on the information gap will not.

I built this tool for buyers. But I think the best agents will end up being its biggest advocates.
