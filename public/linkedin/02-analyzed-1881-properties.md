# I Analyzed 1,881 Spanish Properties. Here Are the Numbers Nobody Talks About.

For the past six months I have been building a dataset of every new build property listed across Spain's three main coastal regions: Costa Blanca, Costa Calida, and Costa del Sol. Not scraping portals -- pulling structured data through an MLS partnership and running each listing through a scoring model.

The dataset now covers 1,881 properties. I want to share what the numbers actually say, because most of it contradicts the narrative you hear from developers and agents.

## The Average Asking Price Is Lower Than You Think

Across all 1,881 listings, the median asking price is around 285,000 EUR. That surprised me. The marketing materials from developers tend to showcase three-bedroom villas at 450,000+ EUR. But the bulk of the market -- and the bulk of the transaction volume -- sits well below that.

Costa Calida is the most affordable region with a median around 195,000 EUR. Costa del Sol is the most expensive at roughly 385,000 EUR. Costa Blanca sits in between, but the spread between north and south is significant. The south is roughly 30% cheaper per square meter than the north.

## Price Per Square Meter Tells a Different Story Than Headline Prices

When you normalize by built area, the ranking shifts. Costa del Sol leads at around 2,800 EUR per square meter. Costa Blanca North follows at approximately 2,400 EUR. Costa Blanca South and Costa Calida cluster around 1,800-2,000 EUR.

But here is the part that matters for investors: the correlation between price per square meter and rental yield is negative. The cheapest regions per square meter produce the highest gross yields. This is not counterintuitive if you think about it -- rental rates do not scale linearly with purchase prices.

## Only 12% of Listings Score Above 70

Our scoring model at avenaterminal.com evaluates every property on a 0-100 scale across value, yield, location, quality, and risk. Of the 1,881 properties in the dataset, only about 225 score above 70. That is 12%.

This does not mean 88% of properties are bad investments. It means that 88% have at least one dimension where they underperform. Maybe the price is fair but the yield is weak. Maybe the yield is strong but the location carries risk. The score is a composite, and most properties have trade-offs.

The properties that score highest tend to share specific characteristics: two-bedroom apartments or townhouses in mid-tier towns, priced 15-25% below the town median, within 3 km of the coast, with communal pools.

## Villas Are Overpriced Relative to Apartments

On a price-per-square-meter basis, villas trade at a 35-45% premium to apartments in the same town. But their rental yields are typically 1.5-2 percentage points lower. The maintenance costs are higher. The occupancy rates are lower because the nightly rate prices out a portion of the rental market.

If you are buying for lifestyle, villas make sense. If you are buying for yield, apartments and townhouses dominate.

## The Construction Status Gap

About 40% of listings in our dataset are off-plan or under construction. These properties typically trade at a 10-15% discount to completed equivalents. That discount partially compensates for the 12-24 month wait and the construction risk, but in a rising market it also represents built-in capital appreciation.

The catch: off-plan properties have higher risk scores in our model because you are buying from a rendering, not a finished product. Developer track record matters enormously here.

## What This Means for Buyers

The data suggests three things:

First, do not start with a region. Start with a budget and a target yield, then see which regions and towns deliver that. The best opportunities often sit in towns you have never heard of.

Second, ignore the headline price. Price per square meter and discount to model estimate are far better indicators of value. Both are available for every listing on avenaterminal.com.

Third, be skeptical of any yield figure that does not account for seasonality and occupancy. A 10% gross yield assumption with 90% occupancy is fantasy on the Costa Blanca in January.
